Maya's Journey
Maya is born with endless potential in a world of possibilities.
Before she can speak or walk, subtle biases begin to shape her future. In some parts of the world, being born female already means facing different expectations.
In some regions, sex ratio at birth is skewed toward boys (e.g., 110.5 males per 100 females in East Asia & Pacific, 2022).
100% potential for financial independence
Maya starts school with excitement, her notebooks pristine and pencils sharpened.
In many countries, Maya has equal access to primary education, but cultural expectations and household responsibilities may already begin to shape her learning experience differently than her male peers.
Primary school enrollment rate for girls globally: 89.7% (World Bank, 2022).
Youth literacy rate (15-24) for females: 90.5% (World Bank, 2022).
Drop-off: In South Asia, girls' primary completion is 87.6% vs. 89.2% for boys (2022).
In rural Nepal, Maya walks two hours to school each day. Her brother stopped attending to help on the family farm, but Maya persists despite also handling household chores after class. Despite her efforts, limited reading materials at home mean she struggles to practice her new literacy skills.
82% on track for financial independence
Maya enters adolescence and secondary school simultaneously—both transformative periods in her life.
As social pressures increase, Maya faces new challenges. In many regions, she navigates safety concerns, early marriage pressures, and increasing household responsibilities that her male classmates don't experience.
Secondary school enrollment rate for girls globally: 77.2% (World Bank, 2022).
Drop-off: 4.1 million more girls than boys are out of school at secondary level in low-income countries (2022).
In Argentina, Maya's teacher notices her aptitude for mathematics but discourages her from pursuing it further, suggesting that language arts might be more "suitable."
65% on track for financial independence
Maya defeats the odds and enters university, facing crucial decisions about her future career path.
Though women like Maya now outnumber men in higher education globally, the fields they study significantly impact their future earnings. Subtle discouragements and stereotypes steer many away from higher-paying STEM fields.
Female tertiary enrollment rate: 43.2% globally vs. 37.8% for males (World Bank, 2022).
Only 36% of students in post-secondary non-tertiary vocational education are female (2022).
Drop-off: Only 34% of STEM students worldwide are female (2022).
Field choice impact: Women-dominated fields lead to 24% lower earnings on average.
In Canada, Maya's computer science class has only 7 women among 35 students. Despite excellent grades, she often doubts whether she belongs in the program.
54% on track for financial independence
Maya enters the workforce with the same degree as her male peers but faces an immediate pay gap.
Despite educational achievements, the transition to employment reveals stark gender differences. Maya encounters gender bias in hiring, negotiation disadvantages, and workplace cultures not designed with women in mind.
Female labor force participation rate: 47.1% globally vs. 72.5% for men (World Bank, 2022).
Young women (15-24) face 1.3x higher unemployment rates than young men in the same age group (2022).
Women with tertiary education face higher unemployment rates than men with equivalent education (2022).
Entry-level pay gap: Women earn 85 cents for every $1 earned by men in their first jobs (2022).
44% of women report experiencing some form of harassment in their first five years of employment (2021).
Women spend 2-10 times more hours on unpaid care work than men, affecting career availability (2022).
Women hold only 28.2% of managerial positions globally (2022).
Only 23% of firms have a female top manager (2022).
In Brazil, Maya discovers her male colleague with identical qualifications was offered a starting salary 12% higher than hers. When she negotiates, she's labeled "aggressive." After work, she rushes home to care for elderly parents while male colleagues network at happy hour.
32% achieve financial independence
Maya has worked for a decade, but the path to leadership remains disproportionately challenging.
The higher Maya climbs, the fewer women she sees. Despite equal or superior qualifications, she faces the "glass ceiling"—invisible barriers created by organizational biases and lack of access to influential networks.
Women hold only 28.2% of managerial positions globally (2022).
Only 23% of firms have a female top manager (2022).
Gender pay gap reaches 19% at mid-career level, widening to 27% at senior levels.
31% of women in leadership positions report having experienced sexual harassment that impacted their career advancement (2021).
Globally, women leave the workforce at 4x the rate of men following childbirth, with 43% not returning within 10 years (2022).
In Kenya, Maya's industry has plenty of women at entry-level, but her company's executive team remains exclusively male. After having a child, she finds herself on a "mommy track" with fewer advancement opportunities, while fathers in her company receive promotions at higher rates.
21% achieve full financial equity

Noah's Journey
Noah enters the world with the same human potential as Maya, his female counterpart.
From his earliest days, subtle societal advantages begin to accumulate. In many societies, simply being born male means greater access to resources and opportunities.
Higher rates of under-5 survival in most regions. Sex ratio at birth favors males in many regions (110.5 males per 100 females in East Asia & Pacific, 2022).
In parts of South Asia, Noah's birth is celebrated with special ceremonies and gifts—traditions not always extended to newborn girls.
100% potential for financial independence
Noah begins his education with expectations weighted in his favor.
While enrollment rates are increasingly equal, Noah's education is often prioritized when family resources are limited. In many contexts, boys receive more teacher attention and encouragement.
Primary school enrollment rate for boys globally: 89.2% (World Bank, 2022).
Youth literacy rate (15-24) for males: 92.8% (World Bank, 2022).
Drop-off: Child labor affects 11.0% of boys ages 7-14 vs. 7.6% of girls globally (2022).
In rural Ethiopia, when times get tough, Noah's family keeps him in school while his sister stays home to help. "He'll need to support a family someday," his parents explain. The community library mostly stocks books that feature male protagonists, subtly reinforcing Noah's sense of belonging in the education system.
80% on track for financial independence
Noah navigates adolescence with different social pressures than his female peers.
While Noah faces his own challenges—including pressure to appear strong and self-sufficient—he generally enjoys greater freedom of movement and fewer domestic responsibilities than girls his age.
Secondary school enrollment rate for boys globally: 77.5% (World Bank, 2022).
Drop-off: Lower retention rates than girls in 71% of middle and high-income countries (2022).
In Mexico, Noah struggles with math but is encouraged to persevere because "boys are naturally good at math." The teacher invests extra time helping him improve.
70% on track for financial independence
Noah chooses his field of study with different influences and expectations than Maya faces.
Though Noah may be less likely to attend university than Maya in some countries, when he does, he's more likely to select higher-paying STEM fields. These choices—shaped by societal expectations—have lasting financial consequences.
Male tertiary enrollment rate: 37.8% globally vs. 43.2% for females (World Bank, 2022).
Drop-off: Men are less likely to complete tertiary education in 104 countries (2022).
Field advantage: Men make up 66% of STEM students, leading to 24% higher earning potential.
In Germany, Noah's engineering program is 80% male. He benefits from numerous same-gender mentors and industry connections, never questioning if he belongs in the field.
60% on track for financial independence
Noah enters the workforce with built-in advantages he may not even recognize.
Noah's transition to employment comes with fewer barriers—from hiring practices that favor male candidates to workplace cultures designed by and for men. These advantages translate into immediate financial benefits.
Male labor force participation rate: 72.5% globally vs. 47.1% for women (World Bank, 2022).
Unemployment rates for young men (15-24): 13.9% globally, 1.3x lower than for young women (2022).
Wage premium: Men earn on average 15% more than women in entry-level positions (2022).
Men spend 1-2 hours daily on household tasks vs. 3-6 hours for women (2022), allowing more time for career advancement.
In Japan, Noah is viewed as a "long-term investment" by his company. He regularly joins senior colleagues for after-hours socializing that builds valuable connections, with no expectation to rush home for family responsibilities.
65% achieve financial independence
Noah's career trajectory faces fewer structural barriers than Maya's path.
As Noah advances professionally, he benefits from homogeneous networks and leadership stereotypes that favor traits associated with masculinity. His path upward, while not guaranteed, has fewer systemic obstacles.
Men hold 71.8% of managerial positions globally (World Bank, 2022).
77% of firms have a male top manager (2022).
Men earn 19-27% more than women at equivalent career levels, widening with seniority (2022).
Fathers see a 6% average increase in earnings after having children, while mothers experience an average 4% decrease (2021).
In the UAE, Noah's assertive communication style is labeled "leadership potential," while the same behavior from female colleagues is often deemed "too aggressive." After becoming a father, colleagues congratulate him and senior leaders begin mentoring him for executive roles.
70% achieve full financial equity

A Tale of Two Journeys

Every child is born with a universe of possibility. But the world they inherit shapes how far they can go.

For Maya, the path is lined with invisible hurdles—expectations, doubts, and double standards. For Noah, the road is paved with encouragement, opportunity, and second chances. What begins as a gentle nudge in childhood becomes a widening gap by adulthood.

Small moments add up: a missed lesson here, a discouraging word there. Maya is told to be careful, to help, to wait her turn. Noah is told to lead, to build, to dream big. These differences, repeated over years, quietly redirect dreams and ambitions until the gap is a chasm.

Behind every statistic is a story: a girl who leaves school too soon, a woman passed over for promotion, a mother whose career stalls while her partner’s soars. By the time Maya and Noah reach their career peaks, the divide is stark. For every 100 men who achieve economic independence, only 30 women do. For every 100 men in leadership, just 28 women stand beside them. Parenthood deepens the split: after children, Maya’s career slows while Noah’s accelerates. Society rewards his ambition and questions hers.

But the gender gap is not inevitable. It is built, brick by brick, by choices and traditions—and it can be dismantled the same way. Every classroom, every workplace, every home offers a chance to close the gap. True equality means more than opportunity; it means changing the rules, the culture, and the expectations that hold Maya back and push Noah forward.

Imagine a world where Maya and Noah walk side by side, their choices shaped only by their passions, not by their gender. That world begins with us.

What will you do today to help close the gap?

Closing the Gap: What We Can Do

  • Early Education: Challenge gender stereotypes in classrooms and learning materials. Encourage girls in STEM subjects and boys in empathy-building activities.
  • Workplace Policy: Implement transparent pay practices, flexible work arrangements, and paid parental leave for all genders.
  • Leadership Development: Create mentorship programs specifically designed to support women's advancement into leadership roles.
  • Shared Care Work: Promote equal distribution of unpaid care work through policy, social norms, and institutional support.
  • Data-Driven Decisions: Continue collecting and analyzing gender-disaggregated data to identify gaps and measure progress.

Breaking Stereotypes in Small Ways

Did you notice that throughout this visualization, we used blue for Maya and pink for Noah—intentionally reversing traditional color associations?

Gender equality isn’t just built through policy changes—it begins with challenging our unconscious biases in everyday decisions. The colors we assign to children, the toys we select, the language we use, and the behaviors we encourage all shape how gender roles evolve.
